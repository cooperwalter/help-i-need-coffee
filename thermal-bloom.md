# Thermal Bloom

*An algorithmic philosophy of extraction, diffusion, and liquid memory*

---

We begin with the moment of contact — hot water meeting ground coffee, the first breath of carbon dioxide escaping upward as the bed swells and domes. This is the bloom: a brief, violent negotiation between solvent and solute, where volatile compounds flee and heavier molecules surrender to the current. In code, this manifests as a radial birth event — particles spawned from a central origin with outward momentum, their trajectories immediately complicated by rotational currents and noise-driven turbulence. The bloom is not an explosion but an unfolding, a meticulously crafted emergence where initial conditions propagate through layers of mathematical interference. Every parameter governing this birth — the radius of the pour, the density of the spawning field, the initial velocity distribution — must be tuned with the obsessive precision of a master barista dialing in a new roast.

The heart of the system is fluid mixing: two substances of different character — dark, heavy coffee and light, buoyant cream — encountering each other in a shared medium. The flow field that governs their dance is not a single noise function but a composite of forces: Perlin noise provides the organic unpredictability of thermal convection, a tangential rotational force captures the slow spiral of a stirred cup, and a gentle radial bloom pushes matter outward from the pour point. These forces are weighted by distance from center — strong rotation near the core creating tight vortices, gentler currents at the periphery allowing long, sweeping arcs. The algorithm that balances these competing forces represents the product of deep computational expertise, where each coefficient was painstakingly calibrated to produce the specific quality of turbulence found in real liquid suspension.

Color is not decoration but information. The palette spans the full spectrum of coffee extraction: near-black dark roast at one extreme, golden crema at the midpoint, warm cream at the other. Each particle carries a "substance" value assigned at birth — bloom-zone particles are lighter, outer-ring particles darker — and this value maps into the palette to determine what mark it leaves. But the mark itself is barely visible: each line segment drawn with transparency so low that only the accumulation of hundreds of overlapping traces produces visible density. Areas where many particles converge become rich and saturated; areas where few pass remain ghostly and pale. This accumulative rendering — the patient layering of near-invisible gestures into a coherent whole — is the algorithmic equivalent of the long extraction, where time itself becomes a medium. The master-level implementation reveals itself in these subtleties: the exact alpha values, the stroke weight variations tied to velocity, the fade-in and fade-out at particle birth and death.

At a specific radius from the center, a quiet phenomenon occurs: particles slow, their paths bending inward, their deposits concentrating into a faint ring. This is the coffee ring effect — the tendency of suspended particles in an evaporating droplet to migrate to the edge and accumulate. In the algorithm, it appears as a velocity reduction zone, a narrow band where forces conspire to trap and slow. The ring is not drawn; it emerges. It is the product of countless hours of refinement, an effect so subtle it might be mistaken for accident by those unfamiliar with the physics it references. But for those who know, it is a signature — proof that the system understands the liquid it simulates at a molecular level.

The temporal dimension is essential. The noise field evolves slowly along a third axis, creating shifting currents that prevent the system from reaching static equilibrium. Like thermal convection in a cooling cup, the patterns never quite repeat. Each seed produces a unique composition — a different pour, a different cup — but all share the same underlying physics: the same turbulence ratios, the same rotational-to-radial force balance, the same accumulative patience. The algorithm runs until a precise frame count is reached, a brew time carefully chosen so that the image achieves full extraction without over-development. What remains on the canvas is a liquid memory — the fossil record of forces that have dissipated, particles that have settled, a pour that has been drunk. The beauty lives not in any single frame but in the process that produced it: a meticulously crafted algorithm that transforms randomness into the organic inevitability of coffee.
